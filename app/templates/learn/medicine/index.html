Bystander first aid

Information sourced from Heart2Heart CPR's First Response: The Complete Guide to First Aid.

Everyone should know first aid. The video I watched discusses what aid you can provide in an emergency situation as a bystander with no medical experience. The content in this section is important no matter who you are. If you decide to work more closely with patients, then you will want to gain deeper exposure into more advanced life support. You have 4 to 6 minutes after breathing has stopped before brain damage. 10+ minutes means death.

If someone suddenly collapses, what do you do? If they are unconscious, breathing inadequately, not breathing, or gasping for air, activate the emergency medical system immediately, retrieve the nearest AED.

First stimulate the patient, applying physical pain. If no response, then check pulse. If no pulse, begin CPR at a rate of 30 compressions to 2 rescue breaths.

AED

An automated external defibrillator (AED) is a portable electronic device that automatically diagnoses life-threatening cardiac arrhythmias of ventricular fibrillation and pulseless ventricular tachycardia and is able to treat them through defibrillation, the application of electricity which stops the arrythmia, allowing the heart to re-establish an effective rhythm.

In order to use an AED, first check the scene and tell a bystander to call 911. Turn the AED on and follow the voice prompts. Remove clothing and attach pads correctly (remove all clothing covering the chest and wipe the chest dry if necessary). Place one pad on the upper right of the chest and place the other pad on the lower left side of the chest, a few inches below the left armpit. If the pads may touch, place one pad in the middle of the chest and the other pad on the back between the shoulder plades. Plug the pad connector cable into the AED if necessary. Stop people from touching the patient and let the AED analyze the heart's rhythm. If shock is advised by the AED, shout "Clear" to inform people to not touch the person, and then hit the Shock button. After the AED delivers the shock or if no shock is davised, immediately restart CPR.

Diagnosis of brain death

Brain death requires presence of 3 conditions: persistent coma, absence of brainstem reflexes, and lack of ability to breathe independently (apnea). Coma is confirmed when a painful stimulus causes no eye opening, no verbal response, and no limb movementin a patient (APGAR score). Brainstem function is assessed by testing multiple reflexes, including pupil responsiveness to light and coughing or gagging with throat suctioning. If coma and basence of brainstem reflexes are confirmed, the final step is an apnea test - temporarily removing a patient from mechanical ventilation and observing for spontaneously breathing. If after 10 minutes no breathing is witnessed and blood CO2 increases by 200mmHg, then patient meets criteria for brain death.
