Solids and liquids

Particles in a solid are tightly packed together and often arranged in a regular pattern; in a liquid, they are close together with no regular arrangement; in a gas, they are far apart with no regular arrangement. Particles in a solid vibrate about fixed positions and do not generally move in relation to one another; in a liquid, they move past each other but remain in essentially constant contact; in a gas, they move independent of one another except when they collide.

The phase in which a substance exists depends on its intermolecular forces and the kinetic energies of its molecules. Intermolecular forces hold the atoms and molecules together, whereas kinetic energy overcomes these attractive forces. Gases can be liquefied by increasing the pressure, which brnigs the molecules of a gas closer together, such that the intermolecular forces become stronger.

All of the attractive forces between neutral atoms and molecules are known as van der Waals forces.

Dispersion forces

One of the three van der Waals forces is present in all condensed phases, regardless of the nature of the atoms or molecules composing the substance, called the London dispersion force, or simply dispersion force. An atom or molecule can develop a temporary, instantaneous dipole if the electrons are distributed asymmetrically, distorting the electrons of a neighboring atom or molecule, producing an induced dipole. This results in a relatively weak electrostatic attraction between the species. These forces are relatively weak and become significant only when the molecules are very close. Larger and heavier atoms and molecules exhibit stronger dispersion forces.

The shapes of molecules also affect the magnitudes of the dispersion forces between them. Longer shapes provide a greater surface area available for contact between molecules, resulting in correspondingly stronger dispersion forces. More compact shapes have weaker dispersion forces.

Dipole-dipole attractions

The positive end of one polar molecule can attract the negative end of another polar molecule, called a dipole-dipole attraction. As an example, HCl has a much higher normal boiling point (188 K) compared to F2 (85 K), since HCl can form dipole-dipole interactions whereas F2 cannot. Boiling and freezing points, or enthalpies of vaporization or fusion, can be used as indicators of relative strengths of intermolecular forces of attractio npresent within different substances.

Hydrogen bonding

A particularly strong dipole-dipole attraction, called a hydrogen bond, occurs when a molecule contains a hydrogen atom bonded to a fluorine, oxygen, or nitrogen atom (the three most electronegative elements). The very large difference in electronegativity between the H atom (2.1) and the atom to which it is bonded (4.0 for an F atom, 3.5 for an O atom, or 3.0 for a N atom), combined with the very small size of a H atom and the relatively small sizes of F, O, and N, leads to highly concentrated partial charges with these atoms. Hydrogen bonding is generally much stronger than dipole-dipole attractions and dispersion forces.

Viscosity

The viscosity of a liquid is a measure of its resistance to flow, determined by the intermolecular forces between the molecules of the liquid, the size and shape of the molecules, and the temperature. The more structurally complex the molecules are and the stronger the intermolecular forces are, the more difficult it is for them to move past each other and the greater the viscosity of the liquid. As the temperature increases, the molecules move more rapidly and thus the viscosity of the liquid decreases.

Cohesive forces

The various intermolecular forces between identical molecules of a substance are examples of cohesive forces. The molecules within the liquid are surrounded by other molecules and are attracted equally in all directions. However, the molecules on the surface of a liquid are attracted only by about one-half as many molecules. Because of this unbalance, liquids contract to form a shape that minimizes the number of molecules on the surface, minimizing surface area, causing a small drop of liquid to assume a spherical shape. Surface tension is defined as the energy required to increase the surface area of a liquid, or the force required to increase the length of a liquid surface by a given amount. This property results from cohesive forces between molecules at the surface, causing the surface of a liquid to behave like a stretched rubber membrane. Among common liquids, water exhibits a distinctly high surface tension due to strong hydrogen bonding, giving it a relatively "tough skin" allowing things like a steel needle or some insects to be able to move on the surface on water even though they are more dense than water.

Adhesive forces

The intermolecular forces between two different molecules are called adhesive forces. Water does not wet waxed surfaces or many plastics because the cohesive forces within the drops are greater than the adhesive forces between the water and the plastic. When water is confined in a glass tube, its meniscus (surface) has a concave shape because water wets the glass and creeps up the side of the tube. On the other hand, the adhesive force between mercury and glass is relatively lower than the cohesive forces between mercury atoms, and so mercury forms a convex meniscus when confined in a tube.

Capillary action

When a liquid flows within a porous material due to the attraction of the liquid molecules to the surface of the material and to other liquid molecules, this is an example of capillary action, where adhesive forces between the liquid and the porous material, combined with the cohesive forces within the liquid, is strong enough to move the liquid upward against gravity. The height to which a liquid will rise in a capillary tube is determined by:\[h=\frac{2T\cos \theta}{r\rho g}\]where \(h\) is the height of the liquid inside the capillary tube relative to the surface of the liquid outside the tube, \(T\) is the surface tension of the liquid, \(\theta\) is the contact angle between the liquid and the tube, \(r\) is the radius of the tube, \(\rho\) is the density of the liquid, and \(g\) is the acceleration due to gravity.

Vapor pressure

The change from the gas phase to the liquid is called condensation. When the rate of condensation becomes equal to the rate of vaporization, neither the amount of the liquid nor the amount of the vapor in the container changes. The vapor in the container is then said to be in equilibrium with the liquid. Keep in mind that this is not a static situation, but a dynamic equilibrium, in which reciprocal processes occur at equal rates. The pressure exerted by the vapor in equilibrium with a liquid in a closed container at a given temperature is called the liquid's vapor pressure or equilibrium vapor pressure. The area of the surface of the liquid in contact with a vapor and the size of the vessel have no effect on the vapor pressure, but they do affect the time required for the equilibrium to be reached. Strong intermolecular forces will favor "recapture" of gas-phase molecules resulting in relatively low vapor pressure (larger molecules, polar molecules, hydrogen bonding, etc.).

As temperature increases, vapor pressure also increases due to increased average kinetic energy of the molecules. At a higher temperature, a greater fraction of molecules have enough energy to escape from the liquid.

Boiling point

When the vapor pressure increases enough to equal the external atmospheric pressure, the liquid reaches its boiling point, the temperature at which its equilibrium vapor pressure is equal to the pressure exerted on the liquid by its gaseous surroundings. For liquids in open containers, this pressure is that due to the earth's atmosphere. The normal boiling point of a liquid is defined as its boiling point when surrounding pressure is equal to 1 atm or 101.3 kPa.

Clausius-Clapeyron equation

The relationship between a substance's vapor pressure and its temperature is described by the Clausius-Clapeyron equation:\[P=Ae^{-\Delta H_\text{vap}/RT}\]where \(\Delta H_\text{vap}\) is the enthalpy of vaporization for the liquid, \(R\) is the gas constant, \(A\) is a constant whose value depends on the chemical identity of the substance, \(T\) is temperature in Kelvin. The equation can be rearranged to compare the vapor pressure of a substance at different temperatures:\[\ln(\frac{P_2}{P_1})=\frac{\Delta H_\text{vap}}{R}(\frac{1}{T_1}-\frac{1}{T_2})\]

Enthalpy of vaporization

Vaporization is an endothermic process. When water on your skin evaporates, it removes heat from your skin and causes you to feel cold. Therefore, the condensation of a gas releases heat.

Melting and freezing

When we heat a crystalline solid, we increase the average energy of its atoms, molecules, or ions and the solid gets hotter. At some point, the added energy allows the particles to break apart and transition into a liquid state, melting the solid. At this point, the temperature stops rising despite the continual input of heat and remains constant until al lthe solid is melted. Only after all of the solid has melted will continued heating increase the temperature of the liquid. The temperature at which the solid and liquid phases of a given substance are in equilibrium is called the melting point of the solid or the freezing point of the liquid.

The amount of heat required to change one mole of a substance from the solid state to the liquid state is the enthalopy of fusion \(\Delta H_\text{fus}\) of the substance. Fusion is an endothermic process. Freezing, the reciprocal process, is an exothermic process.

Sublimation and deposition

Some solids can transition directly into the gaseous state, bypassing the liquid state, via sublimation. The reverse of sublimation is deposition. The formation of frost is an example of deposition; dry ice disappearing without ever turning into a liquid is sublimation. The enthalpy of sublimation \(\Delta H_\text{sub}\) is the energy required to convert one mole of a substance form the solid to the gaseous state.

Sublimation can be modeled as a sequential two-step process of melting followed by vaporization. Applying Hess's law, we can estimate the enthalpy of sublimation by the sum of its enthalpies of fusion and vaporization:\[\Delta H_\text{sub}=\Delta H_\text{fus} + \Delta H_\text{vap}\]

Heating and cooling curves

The relation between amount of heat absorbed or released by a substance and its accompanying temperature change is:\[q=mc\Delta T\]where \(q\) is the amount of heat, \(m\) is the mass of the substance and \(c\) is its specific heat. This relation applies to matter being heated or cooled but not undergoing a change in state. While a substance is undergoing a change in state, its temperature remains constant. In order to find the total heat needed to convert a substance from one temperature to another through a phase change, you must add the heat absorbed/released due to the temperature change as well as the heat of phase change.

For example, to convert ice into steam, you have the equation:\[q_\text{total}=(mc\Delta T)_\text{ice}+n\Delta H_\text{fus} + (mc\Delta T)_\text{water} + n\Delta H_\text{vap}+(mc\Delta T)_\text{steam}\]Noting that the specific heat for ice, water, and steam are all different.

Phase diagrams

A phase diagram combines plots of pressure versus temperature for the liquid-gas, solid-liquid, and solid-gas phase-transition equilibria of a substance.

Notice that the slope for the fusion curve is negative for water whereas the slope for the fusion curve is positive for most other substances. For most substances, if you increase the pressure, you turn liquid into solid as the molecules in the solid are usually closer together. However, the hydrogen bonds in water encourage solid water to be less dense and father apart forming a lattice, so increasing pressure actually forces solid water to become liquid.

The triple point is the point at which all three phases of the substance exist in equilibrium.

Note that carbon dioxide at atmospheric pressure sublimes when temperature is increased.

Supercritical fluids

The critical point is the point where any higher temperature or pressure will cause the substance to become a supercritical fluid, where a gas cannot be liquefied no matter how much pressure is applied. At this point, the boundary between liquid and vapor phases disappears and all of the water is now present in a single phase whose physical properties are intermediate between those of the gaseous and liquid states. Like a gas, a supercritical fluid will expand and fill a container, but its density is much greater than typical gas densities, typically being cloes to that of liquids. They can dissolve nonvolatile soluts, exhibit no surface tension and have very low viscoties, so they can penetrate very small openings in a solid mixture and remove soluble components, making them very useful solvents for a variety of applications.

Solid state of matter

When most liquids are cooled, they eventually freeze and form crystalline solids, in which the molecules are arranged in a definite repeating pattern. If a liquid freezes before its molecules become arranged in an orderly pattern, then it can result in an amorphous or noncrystalline solid (or sometimes a glass). Metals and ionic compounds typically form ordered, crystalline solids, whereas substances that consist of large molecules or a mixture of molecules whose movements are more restricted often form amorphous solids. Some substances such as silicon dioxide can form either crystalline or amorphous solids.

Ionic solids

Ionic solids, such as sodium chloride and nickel oxide, are composed of positive and negative ions held together by electrostatic attractions, which are quite strong. Therefore, they have high melting points. Although they are hard, they are brittle and tend to shatter rather than bend. Ionic solids do not conduct electricity but will conduct if dissolved. Most compounds formed by a metallic element and a nonmetallic element are ionic.

Metallic solids

Metallic solids, such as copper, aluminum, and iron are formed by metal atoms, often described as a uniform distribution of atomic nuclei within a "sea" of delocalized electrons. The atoms are held together by metallic bonding. Metallic solids exhibit high thermal and electrical conductivity, metallic luster, and malleability. Many are very hard and are quite strong. They do not shatter and therefore make useful construction materials. The melting points of the metals vary widely.

Covalent network solid

Covalent network solids, such as diamond, silicon, silicon dioxide (sand), and silicon carbide (carborundum, the abrasive on sandpaper), have networks of covalent bonds. To break the network, covalent bonds must be broken. Therefore, these solids are hard, strong, and have high melting points. Diamond is one of the hardest substances known.

Molecular solids

Molecular solids, such as ice, sucrose (table sugar), and iodine, are composed of neutral molecules, and the strength and melting points of these crystals vary widely.

Properties of solids

A crystalline solid has a precise melting temperature because each atom or molecule of the same type is held in place with the same forces or energy. Ionic crystalline solids are hard, brittle, conduct electricity as a liquid but not as a solid, and have high to very high melting points. Metallic solids are shiny, malleable, ductile, conducts heat and electricity well, and has variable hardness and melting temperature. Covalent network solids are very hard, not conductive, and have very high melting points. Molecular solids have variable hardness, variable brittleness, are not conductive, and have low melting points.

Crystal defects

Occasional defects may occur in the repeating pattern of crystalline solids. Vacancies are defects that occur when positions that should contain atoms or ions are vacant. Less commonly, other atoms occupy these positions, called interstitial sites. Trace amounts of impurities are sometimes added to a crystal (a process called doping) to create defects in the structure that yield desirable changes in its properties. For example, silicon crystals are doped with varying amounts of elements to yield electrical properties for their use in the manufacture of semiconductors and computer chips.

Structures of metals

A pure metal is a crystalline solid with metal atoms packed closely together in a repating pattern. The structure of a crystalline solid, whether a metal or not, is best described by considering its simplest repeating unit, which is referred to as its unit cell, which consists of lattice points that represent the locations of atoms or ions. The entire structure then consists of this unit cell repeating in three dimensions.

Simple cubic structure

When metal atoms are arranged with spheres in one layer directly above or below spheres in another layer, the lattice structure is called simple cubic, and the unit cell is called the simple cubic unit cell or primitive cubic unit cell.

In a simple cubic structure, the spheres are not packed as closely as they could be, and they only "fill" about 52% of the volume of the container. This is relatively inefficient, and only one metal, polonium, crystallizes in a simple cubic structure. The number of particles that each particle in a crystalline solid contacts is known as its coordination number. For a polonium atom in a simple cubic array, the coordination number is six.

Atoms at adjacent corners of the unit cell contact each other, so the edge length of the simple cubic cell is equal to two atomic radii, or one atomic diameter. Since 1/8 of an atom is in each cube, and each cube has 8 of these chunks, there is 1 atom within one simple cubic unit cell. From this reasoning, you can find the edge length and density of polonium's crystal from its atomic radius.

Body centered cubic

Metals that crystallize in an arrangement in which there are atoms at all of the corners and an atom in the center is called a body-centered cubic (BCC) solid. Atoms in the corner do not contact each other but do contact the atom in the center. Each unit cell contains 2 atoms, and an atom in a BCC structure has a coordination number of eight (the corner atoms touch 8 center atoms but do not touch other corner atoms).

Atoms in BCC arrangements are much more efficiently packed, occupying 68% of total volume. Elements or compounds that crystallize with the same structure are said to be isomorphous, and K, Ba, Cr, Mo, W, and Fe are isomorphous with BCC structure.

Face centered cubic

Face-centered cubic (FCC) solids) have atoms at each corner and an atom at the center of each face. Each unit cell contains four atoms (1/8 at each of 8 corners and 1/2 at each of 6 faces). Atoms in FCC arrangement are packed as closely together as possible, occupying 74% of the volume, making the structure also being called cubic closest packing (CCP).

Each atom contacts six atoms in its own layer, three in the layer above, and three in the layer above, giving a coordination number of 12.

Because closer packing maximizes the overall atractions between atoms and minimizes the total intermolecular energy, the atoms in most metals pack in this manner. We find two types of closest packing in simple metallic crystalline structures: CPP, and hexagonal closest packing (HCP). Both consist of repeating layers of hexagonally arranged atoms.

Lattice systems

In general, a unit cell is defined by the lengths of three axes and the angles between them. There are seven different lattice systems, some of which have more than one type of lattice, for a total of fourteen different unit cells.

Structures of ionic crystals

Ionic crystals usually have atoms of different sizes, and so the crystal structure is more complex. Stable structures for ionic compounds result when ions of one charge are surrounded by as many ions as possible of the opposite charge and when cations and anions are in contact with each other. Structures are determined by two principal factors: the relative sizes of the ions and the ratio of the numbers of positive and negative ions in the compound.

In simple ionic structures, we find the anions, which are normally larger than the cations, arranged in a closest-packed array. The smaller cations commonly occupy one of two types of holes (or interstices) remaining between the anions. The four anions surrounding a hole is arranged at the corners of a tetrahedron, so the hole is called a tetrahedral hole. The larger type of hole is found at the center of six anions and is called an octahedral hole. The ratio of octahedral holes to anions in either an HCP or CCP structure is 1:1.

Unit cells of ionic compounds

When an ionic compound is composed of cations and anions of similar size in 1:1 ratio, it typically forms a simple cubic structure. This is like BCC with an atom in the center except the center atom is the cation/anion. When an ionic compound is composed of a 1:1 ratio of cations and anions that differ significantly in size, it typically crystallizes with an FCC unit cell. Sodium chloride, NaCl, is an example of this. We can think of sodium and chloride making the atoms at the vertex of each cube, which makes the larger cube "face-centered."

Calculation of ionic radii

If we know the edge length of a unit cell of an ionic compound and the position of the ions in the cell, we can calculate ionic radii for the ions in the compound if we make assumptions about individual ionic shapes and contacts.

X-ray crystallography

The size of the unit cell and the arrangement of atoms in a crystal may be determined form measurements of the diffraction of X-rays by the crystal, termed X-ray crystallography. Diffraction is the change in the direction of travel experienced by an electromagnetic wave when it encounters a physical barrier whose dimensions are comparable to those of the wavelength of the light. When a beam of monochromatic X-rays strikes a crystal, its rays are scattered in all directions by the atoms within the crystal. When scattered waves traveling in the same direction encounter one another, they undergo interference, changing the intensity depending upon the extent to which the combining waves' maxima are separated. When x-rays of a certain wavelength are scattered by atoms in adjacent crystal planes separated by a distance, they may undergo constructive interference, according to the Bragg equation:\[n\lambda=2d\sin \theta\]

An x-ray diffractometer may be used to measure the angles at which x-rays are diffracted when interacting with a crystal, allowing us to compute distances between atoms.

Solutions and colloids

Solutions

Solutions are defined as homogeneous mixtures of two or more substances. One component is often present at significantly greater concentration, called the solvent. The other components in relatively lesser concentrations are called solutes. The subscript "aq" in a chemical equation signifies that the molecules are solutes and are therefore individually dispersed throughout the aqueous solution (water). Gravity does not cause these dispersed solutes to "settle out" over time.

Water is frequently thought of as the solvent (and is often called the universal solvent because it is capable of dissolving so many different substances). However,, almost any gas, liquid, or solid can act as a solvent. Many alloys are solid sollutions of one metal dissolved in another. Air is a gaseous solution, and you can dissolve oxygen (a gas), alcohol (a liquid), and sugar (a solid) all in water to form a liquid solution.

Solutions are 1) homogeneous, 2) has the physical state of the solvent, 3) have components of a solution dispersed on a molecular scale, 4) will not settle or separate out, and 5) can have varying composition continuously within limits determined by the solubility of the components.

Formation of solutions

Formation of a solution is a spontaneous process, meaning it occurs without requirement of energy from an external source. You can stir a mixture to speed up the dissolution process, but it will dissolve eventually no matter what. Two criteria that favor the spontaneous formation of a solution is a decrease in the internal energy of the system (exothermic change) and an increased dispersal of matter in the system (increasing entropy).

Ideal solutions

When the strengths of the intermolecular forces of attraction between solute and solvent species are no different than those present in separated components, the solution is formed with no accompanying energy change, and such a solution is called an ideal solution. An example is a mixture of ideal gases, since the entities comprising these gases experience no significant intermolecular attractions.

Solvation

Three types of intermolecular attractive forces are relevant to the dissolution process: solute-solute, solvent-solvent, and solute-solvent. First, energy must be consumed to overcome solute-solute and solvent-solvent attractions (endothermic), and released when solute-solvent attractions are established (exothermic process called solvation). The actual spontaneity follows principles of thermodynamics, so a dissolution can be endothermic and still be spontaneous. An example is an instant cold pack, which dissolves \(\ce{NH4NO3}\) with water, causing an endothermic dissolution and absorbing heat from surroundings.

Electrolytes

Substances dissolved in water that turn into ions make up an important class of compounds called electrolytes. Substances that do not yield ions when dissolved are called nonelectrolytes. If the physical or chemical change that generates ions is 100% efficient, then the substance is a strong electrolyte (otherwise it is a weak electrolyte). The strength of the electrolyte can be determined by its ability to conduct electricity.

Ionic electrolytes

The electrostatic attraction between an ion and a molecule with a dipole is called an ion-dipole attraction. When ionic compounds dissolve in water, the ions in the solid separate and disperse uniformly throughout the solution because water molecules surround and solvate the ions, reducing the electrostatic forces between them, representing a physical change known as dissociation. The property of it being a strong electrolyte is independent of how much you dissolve.

Covalent electrolytes

Some covalent compounds such as HCl can be an electrolyte and conduct electricity.

Solubility

The solubility of a solute in a particular solvent is the maximum concentration that may be achieved under given conditions when the dissolution process is at equilibrium. When a solute's concentration is equal to its solubility, it is said to be saturated with that solute. If it's less, then it's unsaturated. If it exceeds the solubility, it is supersaturated (eg. carbonated beverages right after being open). A solution that contains a relatively low concentration of solute is dilute; one with high concentration is concentrated.

Solutions of gases in liquids

For dissolving gases in liquids, there are no solute-solute interactions, so solute-solvent interactions are the sole energetic factor affecting solubility. Water solubility of oxygen is approximately three times greater than that of helium (greater dispersion forces) but 100 times less than chloromethane (dipole-dipole interactions). Higher temperature typically decreases gas solubility (think of it as the gas molecules having more energy to escape), which has large consequences for water ecosystems when higher water temperatures reduce levels of dissolved oxygens. Gas solubility increases as the pressure of theg as increases. For many gaseous solutes, the relation between solubility \(C_g\) and partial pressure \(P_g\) is:\[C-g=kP_g\]where k is a proportionality constant and depends on the identities of the gaseous solute and solvent. This is a mathematical statement of Henry's law: the quantity of an ideal gas that dissolves in a definite volume of liquid is directly proportional to the pressure of the gas.

An example where this is relevant is decompression sickness or "the bends," when the gases dissolved in blood start to escape from the liquid causing bubbles that can cause a variety of symptoms as the diver ascends from high pressure to relatively low pressure.

Solutions of liquids in liquids

Liquids that can be mixed in any proportions to yield solutions have infinite mutual solubility and are said to be miscible. These are typically those with very similar polarities. Liquids that are only slightly soluble are immiscible, and liquids that have moderate mutual solubility are said to be partially miscible.

Solutions of solids in liquids

Solids are generally more soluble with increasing temperature, though there are exceptions. This can be used to prepare supersaturated solutions. For example, a solution can be saturated at a higher temperature, and then temperature can be decreased creating a supersaturated solution. Then precipitation of the excess solute can be initiated by adding a seed crystal or by mechanically agitating the solution, producing heat, such as in hand warmers.

Colligative properties

Some solution properties depend only upon the total concentration of solute species, regardless of their identities. These are colligative properties, and include vapor pressure lowering, boiling point elevation, freezing point depression, and osmotic pressure.

Mole fraction and molality

Molarity (M) is moles of solute over liters of solution. Molarity doesn't work too well when changing temperatures causes changes in the volume of the solution. So a better way to do calculations involving colligative properties is mole fraction, which is ratio of molar amount of something to the total number of moles in solution, and molality, which is moles of solute in kilograms of solvent. These amounts do not vary with temperature and are thus better suited for temperature-independent concentrations.

Vapor pressure lowering

Dissolving a nonvolatile substance in a volatile liquid results in lowering of the liquid's vapor pressure. The presence of solute decreases surface area available to solvent molecules and thereby reduces rate of solvent vaporization. A more rigorous explanation takes into account entropy (dissolving something in liquid increases the microstates available for that liquid to exist, which increases probability of existing in liquid state versus gaseous or solid state). A lower vapor pressures results with a correspondingly higher boiling point.

Raoult's law

The relationship between vapor pressures of solution components and the concentrations of those components is described by Raoult's law: the partial pressure exerted by any component of an ideal solution is equal to the vapor pressure of the pure component multiplied by its mole fraction in the solution:\[P_A=X_AP_A^*\]where \(P_A\) is the partial pressure exerted by component A in the solution, \(P_A^*\) is the vapor pressure of pure A, and \(X_A\) is the mole fraction of A in the solution.

Recalling that the total pressure of a gaseous mixture is equal to the sum of partial pressures for all its components (Dalton's law of partial pressures), the total vapor pressure of a solution is:\[P_\text{solution}=\sum_i P_i=\sum_i X_i P_i^8\]

A nonvolatile substance is one whose vapor pressure is neglibible, and so the vapor pressure above a solution containing only nonolatile solutes is due only to the solvent.

Distillation of solutions

Solutions whose components have significantly different vapor pressures can be separated by a selective vaporization process known as distillation, by heating a solution at different temperatures to vaporize and separate the components. Oil refiners use large-scale fractional distillation to separate the components of crude oil. Crude oil is heated to high temperatures at the base of a tall fractionating column. Vaporized components reach adequately cool zones during their ascent, condense, and are collected.

Boiling point elevation

We discussed that vapor pressure is lowered, and so boiling point is subsequently increased, since boiling point is the temperature at which vapor pressure equals atmospheric pressure. This increase in boiling point is called boiling point elevation and is directly proportional to molal concentration:\[\Delta T_b=K_b m\]where \(K_b\) is the boiling point elevation constant or the ebullioscopic constant, which is dependent on the identity of the SOLVENT, and \(m\) is the molality of all solute species. The identity of the SOLUTE does not matter.

Freezing point depression

Adding salt to a street lowers the temperature at which water turns into ice. This is freezing point depression:\[\Delta T_f=K_f m\]where \(K_f\) is the freezing point depression constant or cryoscopic constant, and \(m\) is the molal concentration of the solute. Again, \(K_f\) depends on the solvent, not the solute.

Osmosis and osmotic pressure of solutions

A number of natural and synthetic materials exhibit selective permeation, meaning that only molecules or ions of a certain size, shape, polarity, charge, and so forth are capable of passing (permeating) through it. These materials are referred to as semipermeable membranes. Diffusion-driven transfer of solvent molecules through a semipermeable membrane is known as osmosis (semipermeable membrane prevents the solute from transferring but allows the solvent).

Say you have two tubes, one with pure solvent and one with a solution. A pure solvent will go through the membrane into a solution, causing the level of the solution to rise, increasing its hydrostatic pressure (weight of column of solution in the tube), resulting in solvent to go back into the pure solvent. When the osmotic pressure and hydrostatic pressure are equal, transfer of solvent is in equilibrium and you have reached the osmotic pressure \(\Pi\) of the solution:\[\Pi=MRT\]where \(M\) is the solute molarity, \(R\) is the universal gas constant, and \(T\) is the temperature.

If you apply pressure to the solution, you reverse osmosis and push solvent molecules from the solution into pure solvent. This is used for desalination of seawater.

Hemolysis

Solutions injected into the body should be isotonic with blood serum, aka they should have the same osmotic pressure as blood. If a less concentrated (hypotonic) solution is injected, water will pass into the blood cells, expand the cells, and rupture them, a process called hemolysis. On the other hand, if the solution is too concentrated (hypertonic), cells will lose water and possibly diein a process called crenation.

Determination of molar mass

Since osmotic pressure and changes in freezing point, boiling point, and vapor pressure are directly proportional to number of solute species present in a given amount of solution, you can measure these properties to find the solute's molar mass.

Colligative properties of electrolytes

So far, we have discussed nonelectrolyte solutions. Observations of colligative properties for electrolytes suggest that ions of sodium chloride and other strong electrolytes are not completely dissociated in solution. To account for this, the van't Hoff factor (\(i\)) is defined as the ratio of solute particles in solution to the number of formula units dissolved. For example, NaCl has a predicted \(i\) of 2 but only a measured \(i\) of 1.9 at a 0.050 molality concentration.

Peter Debye and Erich Huckel proposed a theory to explain the apparent incomplete ionization of strong electrolytes. They suggested that solvation of ions was not complete and some positive and negative ions continue to touch. Thus, the activity, or effective concentration, of any particular kind of ion is less than that indicated by the actual concentration. Ions become more and more widely separated the more dilute the solution, so in extremely dilute solutions, the effective concentrations of the ions are essentially equal to the actual concentrations.

Colloids and suspensions

Suspensions are heterogeneous mixtures composed of relatively large particles that are visible (eg. can be seen with a magnifying glass). They are cloudy and can settle out after mixing. Another class of mixtures is colloids (or colloidal dispersions) which exhibit properties intermediate between those of suspensions and solutions. The particles in a colloid are larger tha most simple molecules but do not settle out upon standing. An example is milk.

Tyndall effect

The particles in a colloid are large enough to scatter light, a phenomenon called the Tyndall effect, making colloidal mixtures appear cloudy or opaque, such as the searchlight beams. Clouds are colloidal mixtures, and so are fog and smoke, which allows bright lights to be seen at night.

Dispersion

The particulate component typically present in a relatively minor amount in a colloid is called the dispersed phase and the substance or solution throughout which the particulate is dispersed is called the dispersion medium.

Preparation of colloidal systems

Colloids are prepared by producing particles of colloidal dimensions and distributing these particles throughout a dispersion medium. Particles of colloidal size are formed by 1) dispersion methods (breaking down larger particles) or 2) condensation methods (growth from smaller units). Gelatin, glue, starch, and dehydrated milk powder are solids that disperse spontaneously and form colloidal systems in contact with water.

An emulsion may be prepared by shaking together or blending two immiscible liquids, breaking one liquid into colloidal size and dispersing through the other liquid. This explains why oil spills can be difficult to clean up, because wave action causes emulsion of oil and water. Emulsions are usually stabilized by an emulsifying agent, a substance that inhibits the coalescence of the dispersed liquid. Milk is an emulsion of butterfat in water, with the proteni casein serving as the emulsifying agent. Mayonnaise is an emulsion of oil in vinegar, with egg yolk components as emulsifying agents.

Clouds are an example of a colloidal system formed from condensation, where water molecules aggregate and form colloid-sized particles. If they coalesce and form large water droplets, that causes rain, sleet, or snow.

Soaps and detergents

Soap was made by boiling fats with a strongly basic solution made by leaching potassium carbonate fro mwood ashes with hot water. Animal fats contain polyesters of fatty acids. When these fats are treated with a base, glycerol and salts of fatty acids are formed. The salts of fatty acids are called soaps. Detergents (soap substitutes) also contain nonpolar hydrocarbon chains. The nonpolar end of a soap or detergent is attracted to oil, grease, or dirt, while the ionic end is attracted by water. So the soap or detergent molecules become oriented at the interface between dirt particles and water. These molecules that have both a hydrophobic and hydrophilic end are called amphiphilic. As a result, dirt particles become suspended as colloidal particles and are washed away. In other words, soaps and detergents makes it so that fats can dissolve in water by turning them into colloids, allowing you to wash the fats away.

Electrical properties of colloidal particles

Dispersed colloidal particles are often electrically charged. All colloidal particles in any one system have charges of the same sign, helping keeping them dispersed. This property can be used to remove them from a variety of mixtures. For example, smoke can be removed by attrating the charged particles to highly charged electrodes, neutralizing them and depositing them as dust.

Gels

Gelatin is a colloid. Gelatin sets on cooling because the hot aqueous mixture of gelatin coagulates as it cools, yielding an extremely viscous body known as a gel. A gel is a colloidal dispersion of a liquid phase throughout a solid phase. The fibers of the dispersing medium form a complex three-dimensional network, with interstices being filled with a liquid medium or a dilute solution of the dispersing medium.
